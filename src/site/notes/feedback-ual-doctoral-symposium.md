_Mahalia, thank you for your presentation. I was interested in the central tension running through the project: how AI might participate in learning and knowledge-making without closing down the uncertainty, contradiction, and unfinishedness that often characterise creative research. Your distinction between LLM reasoning and creative thought feels exciting, while AI systems are frequently designed to resolve, predict, optimise, research often requires us to remain with ambiguity, to sit in and with questions, rather than rush towards answers. There is so much discussion around the ways in which AI focuses on efficiency and assistance, and your project, through epistemic frictions, asks whether a useful research tool might sometimes slow us down, disrupt habitual thinking, or create productive obstacles. In this sense, I wonder whether the supervisor bot can be less a guide (although a poor one), and more a kind of ‘companion in uncertainty’. The idea of PhD-Live and the repositioning of doctoral thinking itself as a public-facing output is interesting. This feels connected to broader questions about liveness, process, and visibility within research culture. What happens when thinking is no longer hidden behind the finished thesis but becomes something that unfolds in public, with all its hesitations, revisions, and unfinished edges? And of ‘intelligence’ itself, if generative systems can persuade us to reconsider what thinking looks like, perhaps they can also invite us to value forms of intuition, association, hesitation, and speculation that sit outside computational logics. Thank you so much._
