#### Chapter outlines:

### 1: introduction

- Frames the research questions: what can AI add to learning and knowledge-making without eroding the generative space creative work depends on, and what assumptions about intelligence and agency are embedded in commercial AI systems entering and becoming prevalent in education.
- Introduces 'After Intelligence' as a layered framing: indicating looking to the future past current hype, as well as the critical interrogation of intelligence as a concept. Names the inversion from the original proposal (designing for others) to the current work (my own practice as site and subject). 
- Introduces PhD-Live and the wider infrastructure briefly. Sets out the thesis structure and explains the distributed autoethnographic and pedagogical approach, so the reader knows what to expect.

---
### 2: methodology, a playful methodology

_Conceptual argument:_

- Positions the research within practice-based and autoethnographic traditions
- Builds a methodological framework through speculative design and design fiction, establishing possibility spaces as a foundational approach, which led to live coding as a playful and performative practice that embodies liveness
- Liveness argued as a methodological commitment, not an aesthetic one
- 'Building with and through' LLM systems as the primary mode of investigation, where each artefact is both a technical object and a theoretical proposition

_Pedagogical grounding:_

- 'Playful' grounded in beliefs about how learning works, drawn from my own teaching practice and experience
- Connects to foundational theories like the Montessori tradition: prepared environments that invite exploration and self-directed making rather than prescribing outcomes. Also connects to creative and art school approaches to teaching that share this orientation (studio pedagogy, crit culture, learning through making as a norm rather than an exception)
- The characteristics of play (experimental, imaginative, self-directed, learning through doing) describe both the research method and my central values about teaching, which is what connects the methodology to the pedagogical thread
- Sicart and Bogost referenced to show awareness of play theory as a field, but the main foundation is practice not theory
- The choice of a playful methodology is a response to the conditions of commercial AI tools narrowing the space of development and exploration by providing swift results

_Autoethnographic account:_

- Occupying the triple role of learner, researcher, and teacher simultaneously, and what that does to methodological choices. Teaching creative computing while researching AI's role in it produces a specific kind of knowledge that neither role alone could generate. I will talk about my practice and positionality here.

---

### 3: intelligence, power and ethics

_Conceptual argument:_

- Traces how intelligence has been defined, measured, and operationalised, and how that history shaped AI development (Crawford, Agüera y Arcas, Hayles, histories of psychometrics and classification)
- The chatbot lineage matters: Weizenbaum built ELIZA as a critique not a product, and that parallel is relevant because the bots in this research interrogate assumptions about intelligence rather than reproduce them
- How these logics enter education through commercial AI systems, and the assumptions about agency, autonomy, and reasoning they carry
- The bots introduced here as critical responses to those assumptions
- The political economy of commercial AI and the power dynamics within institutions that determine how AI gets adopted and who gets to question it

_Pedagogical grounding:_

- When intelligence is defined as measurable and extractable, AI tools built on that logic compress the kinds of learning that resist measurement (tacit, creative, unfinished). Manning on learning otherwise connects here: learning exceeds what intelligence frameworks can capture. 

_Autoethnographic account:_

- Building tools that deliberately resist the resolve-and-optimise logic, and what it means to construct alternative models of intelligence in practice. The echo chamber problem surfaces here not as a technical finding (that's chapter 4) but as a consequence of how LLM 'intelligence' actually works. The sycophancy literature (Cheng et al, Malmqvist) connects: structural tendencies, not incidental failures. 

---

### 4: process, digital gardens, tools for thought, and knowledge infrastructure

_Conceptual argument:_

- Reviews digital gardens, personal knowledge management, tools for thought (Matuschak, Nielsen, Appleton), and the 'second brain' tradition
- Positions the wider infrastructure within this lineage but argues it departs from these frameworks: not productivity tools but constructed instruments for thinking within a specific research practice
- The local research infrastructure documented: supervisor bot, study companion, shared context layer. What they are, how they work, and why they're built as tools for thinking rather than autonomous agents
- The architectural decisions as research positions: local/open-source-first, shared knowledge substrate rather than standalone tools, event renderer paradigm rather than chat interface

_Pedagogical grounding:_

- Tools for thought are fundamentally claims about how learning happens. Matuschak's "How Might We Learn?" bridges tools-for-thought and AI-and-learning. Whose model of learning do these tools encode, and what changes when the learner is also the builder?

_Autoethnographic account:_

- What building and using the infrastructure has surfaced. The echo chamber problem as a finding about correlated context collapsing thinking space. The capability gap between local models and Claude as a finding about the sovereignty/quality tradeoff. The difference between what the tools were designed to do and what they actually revealed

---

### 5: liveness and performance

_Conceptual argument:_

- Driven by the research question: what might a commitment to liveness (keeping knowledge public whilst still forming) offer as a model for learning and knowledge-making in an era of AI-generated outputs?
- PhD-Live as the central object of this chapter: the artefact that embodies the liveness proposition most fully
- Live coding literature anchors the argument here: Cocker on performing thinking in action, McLean et al on the meaning of live (liveness without audience), Soon on executing liveness, Blackwell et al on liveness as durational, embodied, non-repeatable
- The liveness/performance distinction as the central tension: is PhD-Live genuinely performative, or live only in a temporal sense? Does the process need an audience to be a performance, or is the commitment to keeping thinking visible and unfinished enough?
- Creative process traces (Kreminski and Mateas "Reflective Creators," Hammad et al "Tracing Creativity") as a bridge between liveness and documentation: traces imply something left behind, liveness implies something happening now. PhD-Live sits between those two modes
- Commercial AI tends to collapse process into product. Liveness as a critical response: making process visible and keeping it unfinished is a deliberate counter to that collapse

_Pedagogical grounding:_

- What does it mean to learn in public? Keeping knowledge visible while still forming is a pedagogical proposition as much as a methodological one. The liveness commitment models a relationship to knowledge that refuses the finished, polished, AI-generated output as the default mode
- Connects back to the art school and studio pedagogy traditions in chapter 2: showing work in progress, crit culture, the expectation that process is visible and discussable

_Autoethnographic account:_

- What it's actually like to maintain a live public research environment. The vulnerability of thinking in the open, the relationship between the live site and the institutional assessment of the work, and whether the audience (real or imagined) changes the thinking
- The unresolved question of whether PhD-Live is performance or practice, and what that uncertainty itself reveals about liveness as a concept

---

### 6: knowledge, power and ethics

_Conceptual argument:_

- Shifts from how intelligence is defined to what counts as knowledge and who gets to produce it
- creative practice depends on forms of knowledge that can't be fully made explicit: knowing-in-action (Schon), tacit knowledge (Polanyi), embodied expertise (Dreyfus). LLMs work entirely on what can be written down and pattern-matched. This tension matters when connecting AI and creative knowledge-making
- How institutional and commercial knowledge systems encode judgements about what's legitimate, rigorous, or valuable. 
- The politics of knowledge in higher education: whose knowledge counts, what gets credentialled, how AI interacts with existing hierarchies

_Pedagogical grounding:_

- Knowledge-making is a political act, not a neutral process. The question of how AI participates in creative thinking without speaking for the researcher is fundamentally about knowledge and power. Smith-Nunes on offline LLMs and private AI connects the knowledge question to the infrastructure question

_Autoethnographic account:_

- Making knowledge in public via PhD-Live while navigating institutional structures that assess and credential it. The tension between open, unfinished thinking and the institutional demand for legibility. The capability gap between local open sourcs AI versus commercial AI, surfaces here as a finding about knowledge-sovereignty: who controls the infrastructure of your own thinking, and what you give up to maintain that control

### 7: possibility or (im)possibility spaces

_Conceptual argument:_

- A reflective, evaluative chapter: the speculative framing meets what actually happened
- What did the possibility spaces offer, and what happened when speculation met reality?
- The impossibility side: the constrained, material, political reality of higher education. The tension between what can be imagined and what can actually be built given resources, time, precarity, and institutional pressure
- The decision to build locally rather than rely on commercial APIs as both political and practical, shaped by these constraints

_Pedagogical grounding:_

- What kinds of learning become possible or impossible depending on infrastructure, institutional context, and who controls the tools? The overarching context of UK HE being very difficult and shapes everything, including approaches and adaption to AI.
- 
_Autoethnographic account:_

- Building within real constraints (time, capacity, institutional position, limits of local hardware). Breifly reflect on the impossibilities that I have felt in my job as lecturer and as student.
